Avalanche Safety

What is an Avalanche?

Avalanches are a form of mass wasting event where unstable snow suddenly begins sliding down a slope. Avalanches are often triggered by some kind of disturbance and can be very dangerous to skiers, snowboarders and mountaineers particularly in the backcountry. This website means to spread awareness about the dangers and provide an understanding of the complicated mechanics of avalanches and snowpack.

Types of Avalanches

 

Loose Snow Avalanches

These avalanches occur when the snow is loose and not stuck together. They start at a single point and spread outwards in a fan shape down the slope. These types of avalanches are also known as Point Release Avalanches and are generally less dangerous than its slab counterparts.

Dry Loose

Commonly refered to as Sluff by skier and snowboarders, dry loose avalanches are usually not a serious hazard, however, they can knock skiers and snowboarders off balance or drag them into unsafe situations.

Wet Loose

Similar to dry loose, wet loose avalanches occur when heavy wet top snow slides down the slope. These are most common in the spring and later in the day when warmer tempuratures begin melting creating heavy and wet snow.

 

Slab Avalanches

The occur when the upper layers of snow become stuck together to for a cohesive Slab which then releases and slides down the slope. When the avalanche is triggered, the snow releases all at once and all stuck together in big chunks. These are the most dangerous type of avalanche as the result in a very large amount of snow moving extremly quickly. They are able to reach speeds of up to 120km/h and can be triggered in many different ways. Slopes between 35 and 45 degrees are the most likley to experience extreme slab avalanches. These slopes are also the most ideal for skiing and snowboarding which is why slab avalanches are so dangerous to snowsports.

Types of Slab Avalanches:
  1. Wind Slab
  2. Storm Slab
  3. Wet Slab
  4. Persistent Slab
  5. Deep Persistent Slab

What Causes an Avalanche?

Common Avalanche Triggers

Human Activity

90% of avalanches are triggered by the victim or someone in the victims party. Skiers, Snowboarders and Mountaineers are the most common trigger for avalanches

Heavy Snowfall

Rapid accumulation of snow during a snowstorm can lead to excessive weight on the snowpack, leading to instability and possibly an avalanche

Cornice Failure

A Cornice is an overhanging ledge of hardened snow along a ridge formed by wind blown snow. If the cornice colapses and falls onto the slope below, it can trigger and avalanche

Earthquakes

Tremors and vibrations from earthquakes or other geological events can disrupt the snow enough to overcome the strength of a weak layer leading to an avalanche

Windloading

Wind deposites and compacts snow in more sheltered areas creating very dense overlying slabs that are extra unstable and hazardous due to this high density

Avalanche Control

In order to maintain safety, ski resorts use explosives and other methods to trigger avalanches in a safe way before there are any skiers or snowboarders in the area

Snow Characteristics

Importance of Snowpack

Snowpack is the accumulation of Snow Layers that build up on the ground during the winter season. It plays a critical role in avalanche dynamics, especially slab avalanches, as its stability is directly correlated with the avalanche risk. This stability depends on the weather conditions, terrain features and most of all the layers within the snowpack

Snow Layer Strength

Strong Layers in the snowpack referes to snow layers made of snow that sticks to itself. The shape of the snow crystals in strong layers tends to be more rounded rather than the crystals found in Weak Layers which are more faceted, which means the snow does not stick to itself

Snowpack in Slab Avalanches

Slab Avalanches occur when there is a weak layer of snow between two strong layers. The strong layer of snow on top is called the Overlying Slab and the strong layer at the bottom is called the Bed Surface. When the slab avalanche occurs, the weak layer of snow that doesnt stick to itself, isnt strong enough to hold the overlying slab resulting in the overlying slab breaking away and sliding down the slope

 

How Do Weak Layers Form?

On cold clear nights, the surface snow cools down enough to attract moisture from the air around it which then freezes. This forms a layer of frost on the surface of the snow called Surface Hoar that does not stick to itself. Usually this is quickly broken down by sun or wind, however, If there is snowfall directly after these crystals form then the surface hoar gets trapped in the snowpack which can form a very unstable and weak layer below the new snow

Surface Hoar

When there is a strong Tempurature Gradient in the snowpack, facets can develop from the inside leading to weak layers. This gradient must be >1 degree per 10cm for these facets to form. The warmer snow at the bottom of the snowpack will turn directly from solid crsytals to water vapour. This vapour then rises up through the snowpack eventually re-freezing due to the colder tempuratures high in the snowpack leading to weak faceted crystals

Tempurature Gradient

Although rain and warm tempurature are uncommon during the winter season, they do still occur and can cause very unstable weak layers within a snowpack. When the upper layer of snow melts or rain occurs followed by cold tempuratures, this extra moisture freezes and a Melt-freeze Crust forms on the surface of the snow. This is a layer of ice that bonds very poorly with the layers of snow that accumulated on top of it.

Safety Training and Gear

Avalanche Safety Training 1 (AST1)

Avalanche training courses, more specifically Avalanche Skills Training Level 1 (AST1) and Level 2 (AST2), give essential knowledge and experience for anyone looking to explore the backcountry or avalanche-prone terrain. The focus of these courses is safety, decision makiung and situational awareness. On top of being absoluley essential for backcountry, some resorts such as Sunshine Ski Resort in Banff have in-bounds terrain that requires AST1 certification and avalanche gear to access.
