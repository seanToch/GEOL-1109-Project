Avalanche Safety

What is an Avalanche?

Avalanches are a form of mass wasting event where unstable snow suddenly begins sliding down a slope. Avalanches are often triggered by some kind of disturbance and can be very dangerous to skiers, snowboarders and mountaineers particularly in the backcountry. This website means to spread awareness about the dangers and provide an understanding of the complicated mechanics of avalanches and snowpack.

Sluff vs Slab

Types of Avalanches

 

Loose Snow Avalanches

These avalanches occur when the snow is loose and not stuck together. They start at a single point and spread outwards in a fan shape down the slope. These types of avalanches are also known as Point Release Avalanches and are generally less dangerous than its slab counterparts.

Dry Loose

Commonly refered to as Sluff by skier and snowboarders, dry loose avalanches are usually not a serious hazard, however, they can knock skiers and snowboarders off balance or drag them into unsafe situations.

Wet Loose

Similar to dry loose, wet loose avalanches occur when heavy wet top snow slides down the slope. These are most common in the spring and later in the day when warmer tempuratures begin melting creating heavy and wet snow.

 

Slab Avalanches

The occur when the upper layers of snow become stuck together to for a cohesive Slab which then releases and slides down the slope. When the avalanche is triggered, the snow releases all at once and all stuck together in big chunks. These are the most dangerous type of avalanche as the result in a very large amount of snow moving extremly quickly. They are able to reach speeds of up to 120km/h and can be triggered in many different ways. Slopes between 35 and 45 degrees are the most likley to experience extreme slab avalanches. These slopes are also the most ideal for skiing and snowboarding which is why slab avalanches are so dangerous to snowsports.

Types of Slab Avalanches:
  1. Wind Slab
  2. Storm Slab
  3. Wet Slab
  4. Persistent Slab
  5. Deep Persistent Slab
