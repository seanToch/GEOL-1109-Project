Avalanche Safety

What is an Avalanche?

Avalanches are mass-wasting events where unstable snow suddenly begins sliding down a slope. Avalanches are often triggered by some kind of disturbance and can be very dangerous to skiers, snowboarders, and mountaineers, particularly in the backcountry. This website means to spread awareness about the dangers and provide an understanding of the complicated mechanics of avalanches and snowpack

Sluff vs Slab

Types of Avalanches

 

Loose Snow Avalanches

These avalanches occur when the snow is loose and not stuck together. They start at a single point and spread outwards in a fan shape down the slope. These types of avalanches are also known as Point Release Avalanches and are generally less dangerous than their slab counterparts

Dry Loose

Commonly referred to as Sluff by skiers and snowboarders, dry loose avalanches are usually not a serious hazard, however, they can knock skiers and snowboarders off balance or drag them into unsafe situations

Wet Loose

Similar to dry loose, wet loose avalanches occur when heavy wet top snow slides down the slope. These are most common in the spring and later in the day when warmer temperatures begin melting creating heavy and wet snow

 

Slab Avalanches

They occur when the upper layers of snow become stuck together to form a cohesive Slab which then releases and slides down the slope. When the avalanche is triggered, the snow releases all at once and remains in big chunks. These are the most dangerous type of avalanche as they result in a very large amount of snow moving extremely quickly. They can reach speeds of up to 120km/h and can be triggered in many different ways. Slopes between 35 and 45 degrees are the most likely to experience extreme slab avalanches. These slopes are also the most ideal for skiing and snowboarding which is why slab avalanches are so dangerous to snow sports

Types of Slab Avalanches:
  1. Wind Slab
  2. Storm Slab
  3. Wet Slab
  4. Persistent Slab
  5. Deep Persistent Slab

What Causes an Avalanche?

Common Avalanche Triggers

Human Activity

90% of avalanches are triggered by the victim or someone in the victims party. Skiers, Snowboarders and Mountaineers are the most common trigger for avalanches

Heavy Snowfall

Rapid accumulation of snow during a snowstorm can lead to excessive weight on the snowpack, leading to instability and possibly an avalanche

Cornice Failure

A Cornice is an overhanging ledge of hardened snow along a ridge formed by wind blown snow. If the cornice colapses and falls onto the slope below, it can trigger and avalanche

Earthquakes

Tremors and vibrations from earthquakes or other geological events can disrupt the snow enough to overcome the strength of a weak layer leading to an avalanche

Windloading

Wind deposits and compacts snow in more sheltered areas creating very dense overlying slabs that are extra unstable and hazardous due to this high density

Avalanche Control

In order to maintain safety, ski resorts use explosives and other methods to trigger avalanches in a safe way before there are any skiers or snowboarders in the area

Snow Characteristics

Importance of Snowpack

Snowpack is the accumulation of Snow Layers that build up on the ground during the winter season. It plays a critical role in avalanche dynamics, especially slab avalanches, as its stability is directly correlated with the avalanche risk. This stability depends on the weather conditions, terrain features, and most of all the layers within the snowpack

Snow Layer Strength

Strong Layers in the snowpack refers to snow layers made of snow that sticks to itself. The shape of the snow crystals in strong layers tends to be more rounded rather than the crystals found in Weak Layers which are more faceted, which means the snow does not stick to itself

Snowpack in Slab Avalanches

Slab Avalanches occur when there is a weak layer of snow between two strong layers. The strong layer of snow on top is called the Overlying Slab and the strong layer at the bottom is called the Bed Surface. When the slab avalanche occurs, the weak layer of snow that doesn't stick to itself isn't strong enough to hold the overlying slab resulting in the overlying slab breaking away and sliding down the slope

 

How Do Weak Layers Form?

On cold clear nights, the surface snow cools down enough to attract moisture from the air around it which then freezes. This forms a layer of frost on the surface of the snow called Surface Hoar that does not stick to itself. Usually, this is quickly broken down by sun or wind, however, If there is snowfall directly after these crystals form then the surface hoar gets trapped in the snowpack which can form a very unstable and weak layer below the new snow

Surface Hoar

When there is a strong Temperature Gradient in the snowpack, facets can develop from the inside leading to weak layers. This gradient must be >1 degree per 10cm for these facets to form. The warmer snow at the bottom of the snowpack will turn directly from solid crystals to water vapor. This vapor then rises up through the snowpack eventually re-freezing due to the colder temperatures high in the snowpack leading to weak faceted crystals

Temperature Gradient

Although rain and warm temperature are uncommon during the winter season, they do still occur and can cause very unstable weak layers within a snowpack. When the upper layer of snow melts or rain occurs followed by cold temperatures, this extra moisture freezes and a Melt-freeze Crust forms on the surface of the snow. This is a layer of ice that bonds very poorly with the layers of snow that accumulated on top of it

Avalanche Risk

Testing The Snowpack

 

Testing the snowpack is essential for avalanche risk assessment. Multiple tests are conducted to check for any weak layers or instability. To perform these tests, a pit is dug that shows the cross-section of the snowpack. At this point, the temperatures at the bottom and top of the snowpack are measured to check the thermal gradient

Snowpack Tests

  1. Compression Test - Pressure is applied to the top of an isolated column of the snowpack until it collapses. This is done to determine stability and any weak layers present
    2.  
  2. Extended Column Test - Similar to the compression test but done with a larger section of te snowpack to get better insight into the stability across a wider area
    3.  
  3. Rutschblock Test - Checks how easily a block of snow can be released to determine how prone to sliding the layer is
    4.  
  4. Shear Test - Similar to the pressure test but focuses more on specific layers to check how easily they shear off

Safety Training and Gear

Avalanche Safety Training 1 (AST1)

Avalanche training courses, more specifically Avalanche Skills Training Level 1 (AST1) and Level 2 (AST2), give essential knowledge and experience for anyone looking to explore the backcountry or avalanche-prone terrain. The focus of these courses is safety, decision-making, and situational awareness. On top of being essential for backcountry, some resorts such as Sunshine Ski Resort in Banff have in-bounds terrain that requires AST1 certification and avalanche gear to access

On top of training, avalanche gear is essential for safety in the backcountry. The key pieces of gear are a Transiver which is used to locate someone buried in snow, a Probe which is used to pinpoint the exact location of the buried person, and a Shovel to dig. Additionally, Airbags can keep you on the surface of the snow in the event of an avalanche and First Aid Kits are also valuable for increasing safety in the backcountry
